Headline CPI

The headline
UAE inflation has travelled from +5% to −2.7% to +7.9% in a decade. Four distinct macro regimes, twenty-four consecutive months of deflation (2019–2020) — a GCC record — and a 10-point swing between trough and peak.
What to notice: The 2018 VAT shock is a spike, not a regime — fully reversed within 12 months. The 2019–2020 deflation was structural (property oversupply). The 2022 spike was external (global energy + supply chains). Each regime has a different driver, which is why a single forecasting rule doesn't work — and why the regime-discovery tab matters.
Method: Chained 2014-base and 2021-base FCSA CPI series using the published Jan-2021 MoM on the new base as linking factor. Validated to ±0.01 pp against FCSA's own published YoY.

Sector Composition

The headline
Headline inflation = weighted blend of sector moves. Housing (dark navy, 34% basket weight) is the persistent base contributor across 2023–2025. Transportation (teal, 14.6% weight) is the volatile amplifier — it drove the 2022 oil spike and the mid-2023 crash.
Why it matters: Procurement and pricing teams care about different sectors than the headline index does. A supermarket sees headline dominated by Housing they don't sell. The decomposition lets you filter to the sectors that matter for your P&L — and see which sectors are pressuring you vs. the broader market.
Method: Contribution = sector_YoY × basket_weight / 100. Contributions sum to headline up to a small non-linearity residual. Weights from FCSA 2021-base basket.

Regime Map

The headline
HDBSCAN on z-scored 13-sector profiles independently rediscovers the 2018 VAT shock, 2020 COVID deflation, 2022 oil spike, and current 2024–2025 regime — without any supervision or pre-specified cluster count. Current state (Dec 2025) sits in its own distinct cluster.
Why unsupervised matters: Hand-labeling regimes bakes in analyst bias. HDBSCAN produces the same 4 macro eras an economist would identify, plus 5 finer micro-regimes an economist would miss. Hover any point to see which month it represents; points close together = similar inflation fingerprint.
Method: 13 sectors × 144 months z-scored. HDBSCAN with min_cluster_size=6 identifies 9 regimes + 20 transitional months. UMAP reduces to 2D for visualisation (neighbors=10, min_dist=0.1).

Forecast Timeline

The headline
Ridge regression catches the 2022 spike 3 months early, tracks the mid-2023 dip, and stays within ~1pp of actual through 2024–2025. The naive seasonal baseline (grey dashed) is visibly lost — it confuses the 2020 deflation with a 2024 forecast.
The decision value: At 90% directional accuracy with a 3-month lead, this is enough signal to shift hedging strategy — for a retailer repricing quarterly, or a developer locking material costs, that's the difference between acting before the move and reacting after it.
Method: Ridge on lagged sector YoYs at lags {1, 2, 3, 6, 12}. Walk-forward expanding-window backtest: at each origin, refit on data up to that date and predict 3 months ahead. No look-ahead bias. 71 out-of-sample origins, 2020–2025.

Model Benchmark

The headline
Ridge wins on RMSE at 1 and 6-month horizons. XGBoost matches Ridge on directional accuracy but overfits on RMSE. The naive seasonal baseline has negative R² at every horizon — predicting this year's inflation from last year's same month is worse than predicting the mean.
Why Ridge wins: 144 monthly observations × ~70 features — the n/p ratio favors linear shrinkage. XGBoost has the capacity to overfit on this sample size. With 5+ years more data, that calculus changes. The fact that Ridge beats XGBoost is the signal — not a weakness.
Method: Three models compared: Naive (YoY_{t+h} = YoY_{t−12+h}), Ridge (L2-regularised linear), XGBoost (300 trees, max_depth=3). All three evaluated on identical 71 walk-forward origins per horizon.
